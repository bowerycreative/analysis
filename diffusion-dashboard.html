About the Diffusion of Innovations framework ▼

The Diffusion of Innovations is a theory developed by sociologist Everett Rogers in 1962 to explain how, why, and at what rate new ideas and technologies spread through populations. Rogers identified five adopter categories — Innovators, Early Adopters, Early Majority, Late Majority, and Laggards — each representing a distinct segment of the population based on their willingness to adopt something new.

The S-shaped cumulative adoption curve reflects a consistent empirical pattern: adoption starts slowly among risk-tolerant innovators, accelerates as social proof builds through the majority, then slows as only the most resistant laggards remain. This curve has been replicated across thousands of studies spanning agriculture, technology, medicine, and consumer products.

For marketing purposes, the curve is useful because it maps directly to customer acquisition cost. Early in a brand's life, the easiest-to-convert customers adopt first — CAC is low. As the addressable pool matures, the remaining unconverted population requires more exposure, more proof, and more spend to convert — CAC rises structurally, independent of campaign performance.

Rogers, E.M. (1962). Diffusion of Innovations. Free Press of Glencoe. Now in its 5th edition (2003).
